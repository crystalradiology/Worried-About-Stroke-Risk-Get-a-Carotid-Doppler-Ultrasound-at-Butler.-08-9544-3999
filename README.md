What is a Carotid Doppler ultrasound?
A Carotid Doppler ultrasound is a specialised imaging test used to assess blood flow in the carotid arteries, which are located on either side of the neck. These arteries supply oxygen-rich blood to the brain.
The test uses sound waves to create real-time images of blood flow, helping doctors identify:
•	Narrowing (stenosis) of arteries
•	Blockages caused by plaque buildup
•	Abnormal blood flow patterns
When patients Get Carotid Doppler ultrasound at Butler, they receive accurate and detailed imaging that plays a critical role in stroke prevention.
How is a Carotid Doppler Ultrasound Performed?
A carotid Doppler ultrasound is a simple, painless, and non-invasive procedure.
Here’s what typically happens during the scan:
•	A sonographic gel is applied to the neck area
•	A small handheld device called a transducer is placed near the carotid arteries (beside the Adam’s apple)
•	The sonographer gently moves the probe to capture images
•	Mild pressure may be applied to obtain clearer results
The unique feature of Doppler ultrasound is that it shows blood flow as a colour-coded, moving image, allowing specialists to:
•	Measure the velocity of blood flow
•	Detect turbulence or irregular flow
•	Identify the degree of narrowing in the arteries

How Carotid Arteries Affect Stroke Risk
The carotid arteries are a common source of blood clots, especially when there is narrowing due to atherosclerosis (plaque buildup).
This condition is known as carotid artery stenosis.
When arteries narrow:
•	Blood flow to the brain is reduced
•	Clots can form more easily
•	The risk of stroke increases significantly
Doctors may sometimes detect this narrowing by hearing a carotid bruit (a “whooshing” sound) using a stethoscope.
A Doppler ultrasound confirms the diagnosis and measures the severity of the blockage.
Who Should Consider This Scan?
You may be advised to undergo a carotid Doppler ultrasound if you:
•	Have experienced symptoms of a TIA or mini-stroke
•	Have a history of stroke or cardiovascular disease
•	Have high blood pressure, diabetes, or high cholesterol
•	Smoke or have a family history of vascular disease
•	Have been diagnosed with carotid bruit
•	Need monitoring of known arterial narrowing
